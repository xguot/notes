* The Spectral Theorem
The Spectral Theorem dictates that any real symmetric or complex Hermitian matrix $S$ can be orthogonally diagonalized.
The operator is completely defined by its spectrum of real eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ and orthonormal eigenvectors $q_i$,

$$\boxed{S = Q \Lambda Q^\dagger}$$

The columns of $Q$ form an eigenbasis for the space.
The diagonal matrix $\Lambda$ contains the real eigenvalues.
In quantum mechanics, this guarantees that physical observables (Hermitian operators) yield strictly real measurement outcomes.

* Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
SVD generalizes the spectral theorem to any arbitrary $m \times n$ matrix $A$,

$$\boxed{A = U \Sigma V^\dagger}$$

The $m \times m$ unitary matrix $U$ contains left singular vectors.
The $n \times n$ unitary matrix $V$ contains right singular vectors.
The diagonal matrix $\Sigma$ contains non-negative real singular values $\sigma_i$.
By constructing the symmetric matrix $A^\dagger A$, we map SVD directly back to the Spectral Theorem,

$$A^\dagger A = (V \Sigma U^\dagger)(U \Sigma V^\dagger) = V \Sigma^2 V^\dagger \, .$$

The right singular vectors $V$ are the exact eigenvectors of $A^\dagger A$.
The singular values are the exact square roots of its eigenvalues, $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$.

* Difference Matrices and the Discrete Laplacian
For a finite difference matrix $D$ approximating the first derivative $\frac{d}{dx}$,
the matrix transpose $D^T$ acts as the negative adjoint derivative $-\frac{d}{dx}$.
Their product forms the symmetric discrete Laplacian operator,

$$D^T D \approx -\frac{d^2}{dx^2} \, .$$

Computing the SVD of $D$ diagonalizes this Laplacian without requiring its explicit construction.
The right singular vectors $v_i$ correspond exactly to pure discrete Fourier modes.
Because the first derivative of a pure mode strictly forces a 90-degree phase shift (changing even parity to odd parity),
the output cannot reside in the same geometric space as the input.
The SVD perfectly captures this transformation by mapping the input to an entirely new, orthogonal output basis $u_i$,

$$D v_i = \sigma_i u_i \, .$$

This is the exact discrete equivalent of the continuous Fourier derivative $\frac{d}{dx} e^{ikx} = i k e^{ikx}$.
The input mode $v_i$ acts as the pure wave.
The transition to the orthogonal basis $u_i$ executes the 90-degree geometric rotation (the $i$).
The singular value $\sigma_i$ represents the gradient magnitude (the $k$).
Low-frequency smooth modes possess minimal gradients, yielding $\sigma_i \approx 0$.
Highly oscillatory modes possess steep gradients, mapping aggressively to large $\sigma_i$.

* Schmidt Decomposition (Quantum Bipartite States)
Consider a pure bipartite quantum state expanded in arbitrary local bases,
forming a complex coefficient matrix $a_{i\mu}$,

$$|\psi\rangle_{AB} = \sum_{i,\mu} a_{i\mu} |i\rangle_A \otimes |\mu\rangle_B \, .$$

Applying SVD to the coefficient matrix yields $a = U \Sigma V^\dagger$.
Because $U$ and $V^\dagger$ are unitary, they execute valid local basis transformations on System A and System B.
This diagonalizes the coefficient matrix, collapsing the state into the Schmidt decomposition,

$$\boxed{|\psi\rangle_{AB} = \sum_i \sqrt{p_i} |i\rangle_A \otimes |i'\rangle_B}$$

The singular values $\Sigma_{ii}$ act as the Schmidt coefficients $\sqrt{p_i}$.
Because $a a^\dagger$ corresponds to the reduced density operator $\rho_A$,
the squared singular values $\sigma_i^2$ are exactly the classical probabilities $p_i$ of the local subsystems.
