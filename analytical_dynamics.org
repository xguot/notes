* D'Alembert's Principle and Virtual Work
Consider a system of particles subject to a constraint surface defined by $f(x, y, t) = 0$.
A physical differential representing actual motion evolves over time as

$$df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} dy + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} dt = 0.$$

By freezing time ($\delta t = 0$), we define an infinitesimal virtual displacement $\delta \mathbf{r} _ i$ satisfying

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \delta x + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \delta y = 0.$$

The principle of virtual work dictates that the constraint forces $\mathbf{F} _ {ci}$ do no work,

$$\sum _ i \mathbf{F} _ {ci} \cdot \delta \mathbf{r} _ i = 0.$$

To distinguish this from physical work, consider a bead constrained to a track on a rotating wheel.
For a real displacement $d\mathbf{r}$, the wheel rotates over time $dt$, allowing the moving track to perform physical work.
For a virtual displacement $\delta\mathbf{r}$, the rotation is kinematically frozen ($\delta t = 0$), the track is instantaneously stationary, and its reactive force acts strictly normal to the virtual path.

Introducing the applied forces $\mathbf{F} _ {ai}$ and the inertial response $\dot{\mathbf{p}} _ i$, D'Alembert's principle establishes the fundamental dynamical relation by equating the system to a state of dynamic equilibrium,

$$\boxed{ \sum _ i (\mathbf{F} _ {ai} - \dot{\mathbf{p}} _ i) \cdot \delta \mathbf{r} _ i = 0 }.$$

* The Lagrangian and Action
To extract the kinetic structure, integrate D'Alembert's principle over time and apply integration by parts to the inertial term.
The vanishing boundary conditions allow shifting the time derivative from the momentum to the variation, yielding

$$-\int \dot{\mathbf{p}}_i \cdot \delta\mathbf{r}_i \, dt = \int \mathbf{p}_i \cdot \delta\dot{\mathbf{r}}_i \, dt \, .$$

Substituting Newtonian momentum $\mathbf{p}_i = m_i\dot{\mathbf{r}}_i$, the integrand becomes $m_i \dot{\mathbf{r}}_i \cdot \delta\dot{\mathbf{r}}_i$.
By the standard calculus identity for variations, $v \cdot \delta v = \frac{1}{2} \delta(v^2)$, this rewrites exactly as

$$\int \delta \left( \frac{1}{2} m_i \dot{\mathbf{r}}_i^2 \right) dt = \delta \int T \, dt \, .$$

Grouping this emergent kinetic term with the potential energy $V$, the evolution of the system is governed by Hamilton's principle of stationary action,

$$\delta \int _ {t_1} ^ {t_2} (T - V) \, dt = 0 \, .$$

* Euler-Lagrange Equations
Applying the calculus of variations to the action integral with fixed endpoints yields the equations of motion in configuration space,

$$\boxed{ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q} _ i} \right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q _ i} = 0 }.$$

* The Legendre Transformation
To transition from configuration to phase space, the Legendre transformation maps $f(x)$ to a dual function $g(u)$ by exchanging $x$ for its derivative:

$$u \equiv \frac{df}{dx}$$

Inverting this mapping yields the constraint $x(u)$, which is substituted to eliminate $x$ entirely:

$$g(u) = u x(u) - f(x(u))$$

Evaluating the total differential of $g = ux - f$ reveals why this works:

$$dg = x \, du + u \, dx - df$$

By definition, $df = u \, dx$.
Substituting this perfectly cancels the $dx$ terms:

$$dg = x \, du$$

This proves $g$ is strictly a function of $u$ and yields the symmetric relation:

$$x = \frac{dg}{du}$$

* Hamiltonian Mechanics
We apply the Legendre transformation to the Lagrangian $L(q, \dot{q}, t)$ to replace the generalized velocities $\dot{q} _ i$ with their conjugate momenta $p _ i$.
The conjugate momentum is formally defined as

$$p _ i = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q} _ i}.$$

We construct the Hamiltonian $H(q, p, t)$ as the Legendre transform of $L$,

$$H(q, p, t) = \sum _ i p _ i \dot{q} _ i - L(q, \dot{q}, t).$$

Evaluating the total differential $dH$ yields

$$dH = \sum _ i (\dot{q} _ i \, dp _ i + p _ i \, d\dot{q} _ i) - \left( \sum _ i \frac{\partial L}{\partial q _ i} \, dq _ i + \sum _ i \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q} _ i} \, d\dot{q} _ i + \frac{\partial L}{\partial t} \, dt \right).$$

Enforcing the momentum definition and the Euler-Lagrange relation $\dot{p} _ i = \frac{\partial L}{\partial q _ i}$, the terms proportional to $d\dot{q} _ i$ vanish identically, leaving

$$dH = \sum _ i (\dot{q} _ i \, dp _ i - \dot{p} _ i \, dq _ i) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial t} \, dt.$$

Equating this result with the formal multivariable expansion of $H(q, p, t)$ uniquely determines Hamilton's equations of motion,

$$\boxed{ \dot{q} _ i = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p _ i}, \quad \dot{p} _ i = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q _ i}, \quad \frac{\partial H}{\partial t} = -\frac{\partial L}{\partial t} }.$$
